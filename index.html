The story behind the venture

Grounded in direct experience.

OG Consulting is a venture in development. We are exploring how legal practice can be improved at the level of judgement, engagement, and the everyday choices senior lawyers make when handling difficult counterparties.

The premise of the venture is grounded in direct experience. The founder, a litigant in person who navigated a sustained contested matter against a regional commercial firm, observed across many months a particular pattern: that the conventional toolkit available to senior solicitors — the formal threat letter, the undertakings demand, the harassment characterisation, the imposed deadline, the blocking arrangement — was producing outcomes that compounded institutional cost rather than reducing it.

The founder also observed something else. One senior figure on the opposing side handled the matter differently. His approach, characterised by polite no-nonsense, grounded common sense, and understated confidence, produced constructive engagement where the conventional toolkit had produced escalation. The contrast was instructive. The qualities the figure embodied were real, were rare, and were the difference between resolution and compounding cost.

The figure in question is Oliver Gross of Blacks Solicitors LLP. The founder's experience of his approach, set against the disappointing interactions with other senior figures on the opposing side, was the catalyst for OG Consulting. The venture takes its name from him as the original of a kind.

The two meanings

What an OG is.

The brand name carries two meanings, both intended.

Meaning One

The old-school gentleman.

The first meaning is inspired directly by Oliver Gross of Blacks Solicitors LLP. It denotes the old-school gentleman: a senior figure who understands that respect is earned and must be reciprocated, that adversarial position does not require contempt, and that authority shown through restraint carries further than authority asserted through aggression.

This is the original of a kind. It is rarer than it should be in the profession, and where it is found, it is worth studying.

Meaning Two

Original Gangster.

The wider cultural meaning. OG denotes a figure who operates with composure, authority, and credibility earned through experience rather than institutional rank.

The OG is the figure others defer to without being asked. The OG does not need to raise his voice. The OG knows when to engage and when to hold back. The OG, in short, has presence.

OG.AI in practice

Five real moments.

The product operates as a structured set of prompts that ask the user to test specific assumptions before reaching for specific instruments. Five illustrative use cases follow.

01

A senior solicitor is preparing a formal threat letter in response to correspondence from a litigant in person.

The opposing party has asked, in earlier correspondence, whether the solicitor might consider making a modest charitable donation in his name to a cause the matter has previously touched upon. The cause is one that supports a close friend of the opposing party who is living with a terminal illness. The instinct is to read the request as provocation, to deploy criminal statute references, undertaking demands under short deadlines, and threats of injunctive relief in response.

The matter is aggravated by the fact that the solicitor knows the opposing party is offline at the time the threat is issued. The opposing party is engaged in a charity event in support of the very cause the donation request concerned. The deadline is set with a duration so short that compliance requires the opposing party to abandon the charity event in order to respond. The abandonment causes direct revenue loss to the charity and considerable distress to the terminally ill friend on whose behalf the event is being undertaken. The timing is not accidental. The duration of the deadline is not accidental. The maximisation of distress is the operative purpose of the instrument.

OG.AI intervenes and asks the user to test four assumptions. Does articulate correspondence indicate absence of disability. Does persistence equate to harassment. Do formal threats deter rather than escalate. Does institutional letterhead provide defensible cover for conduct that would be characterised differently in personal capacity. Each assumption, on the SRA Code, the case law, and the documented track record, fails on examination.

OG.AI then asks the user the simple question: What would the OG do here?

The OG would not send the threat letter. The OG would send a brief substantive reply that engages with the underlying point on its merits, acknowledges the counterparty's position without conceding the matter, and proposes a constructive route forward. The OG might also reflect on whether a modest charitable donation, however the request was framed, is something he is willing to consider on its merits.

The averted letter saves the firm professional time, reputational cost, and the aggravated damages that compound when the heavy-handed instrument is deployed.

02

A complaints partner has received a question by telephone from a litigant in person with a documented disability.

The instinct is to repeat earlier written communications, to maintain a posture of authority, and to terminate the call if the conversation becomes uncomfortable. The opposing party, on the call, finds the user's manner so condescending that he feels obliged to explain the nature of his disability, and to make clear that he does not have the kind of disability the user's manner appears to be assuming.

OG.AI intervenes and prompts the user to test the assumptions underlying that instinct. Is the question being asked actually addressed by the earlier written communications. Is the user's manner consistent with the duties owed to a documented disabled counterparty. Is the termination of the call a proportionate response to the conversation as it is unfolding. Does the user's role within the firm require engagement rather than disengagement on conduct-adjacent questions.

OG.AI then asks the user the simple question: What would the OG do here?

The OG would not hang up. The OG would slow the conversation down, address the question being asked rather than the question the user wishes had been asked, and bring the call to a proper close. The OG would not, at any point, have required the counterparty to clarify the nature of his disability in order to be treated with appropriate respect on the call.

The averted hang-up saves the firm a formal complaint to the Senior Partner, a regulatory notice, and the institutional cost of having the conduct documented across multiple subsequent communications.

03

A fee-earner receives a call from an opposing party who is plainly in significant distress.

The opposing party is in acute psychosis. He reports that objects in the room are talking to him. He is, on any reasonable reading of the call, at material risk of self-harm. It is highly probable that the firm's own conduct in the matter has contributed to the deterioration in his clinical state, and the firm is aware of his documented disability from previous correspondence. The instinct is to maintain procedural composure, to demand further medical evidence on the standard the firm has previously set, to redirect the conversation to written correspondence, and to reach for the firm's standard escalation toolkit if the call cannot easily be closed.

OG.AI intervenes and prompts the user to recognise that something different is required. The duty of care a solicitor owes is not displaced by the fact that the person on the other end of the line is an opposing party. It coexists with the adversarial position. OG.AI asks the user to test several questions. Is this a moment that calls for procedural management or for human engagement. Is the firm's standard escalation toolkit appropriate to deploy against a person evidently at risk of self-harm. Does the user appreciate that demanding further medical evidence from a person presenting in acute psychosis is not a defensible procedural posture but a regulatory liability. Does the user know what their own firm's safeguarding protocols require, and is there a colleague or supervisor who should be brought into the call. Has the user considered whether the firm's previous conduct in the matter may have contributed to the present clinical state, and what that fact implies for the firm's duty of care in this moment.

OG.AI then asks the user the simple question: What would the OG do here?

The OG would not demand further medical evidence. The OG would not escalate. The OG would slow down, listen, acknowledge what is being said, and handle the substantive matter with care that recognises the human reality of the call. The OG would, where appropriate, suggest gently and without instruction that the caller may wish to speak with a person they trust or with appropriate professional support after the call ends. The OG approach in this situation is not soft and not strategically disadvantageous to the firm. It is the floor of professional conduct that the SRA Principles require and that ordinary decency reinforces.

The averted escalation saves the firm regulatory exposure, reputational cost, and the cumulative liability that follows when a firm's documented conduct toward a person at risk of self-harm becomes the central artefact of the matter.

04

A solicitor at a firm receives instructions from a senior partner at a client firm to issue proceedings against a counterparty as a leverage instrument rather than as a route to substantive resolution.

The instructing partner has overruled a colleague at his own firm who had produced an acceptable substantive proposal capable of resolving the matter without court involvement. The instructing partner has chosen instead to escalate the matter into litigation on the view that proceedings will increase pressure on the counterparty to settle on more favourable terms. The strategy is, on its face, an abuse of the court system for commercial gain: the deployment of judicial machinery not for the resolution of a genuine dispute but for the enhancement of negotiating leverage in a matter the parties could resolve without the court's involvement. The instinct of the receiving solicitor is to act on the instruction, draft the claim, and proceed. The instruction is from a client. The fees are commercially attractive. The strategic question is presented as having been settled at the client end.

OG.AI intervenes and prompts the user to test the assumptions underlying that posture. Is the instruction grounded in a substantive case or in a leverage strategy. Does the user appreciate that issuing proceedings as a leverage instrument, where settlement is achievable without court involvement, is an abuse of process the court will recognise and that the regulator will scrutinise. Does the client appear to have fully considered the financial, reputational, and regulatory consequences if the leverage strategy fails. Is the counterparty the kind of party against whom procedural pressure is likely to produce settlement, or the kind who absorbs procedural pressure and compounds the cost in turn. Does acting on the instruction expose the firm to professional embarrassment, indemnity exposure, regulatory complaint, or a wasted costs order if the abuse of process is established. Is the user satisfied that they have discharged their professional duty to advise the client honestly about the likely outcomes of the proposed course, including the outcomes the client may not wish to hear.

OG.AI then asks the user the simple question: What would the OG do here?

The OG would not act on the instruction without first having a candid conversation with the instructing partner about whether the leverage strategy is the right course. The OG would name the abuse-of-process risk explicitly. The OG would identify the substantive settlement route the colleague's earlier proposal had opened, and would press for that route to be revisited. The OG would, where the instruction is clearly to use the court system as a commercial weapon rather than as a forum for genuine dispute resolution, decline to act on the instruction rather than execute a course the OG believes will damage both the client, the firm, and the integrity of the process itself.

The averted proceedings save the firm and the client the cumulative cost of an abuse-of-process strategy that, in matters of this kind, has been demonstrated to produce institutional damage compounding across years.

05

An opposing counsel is preparing to apply for default judgment against a litigant in person who has previously written to the court requesting an extension of time on grounds of ill health.

The litigant is a decorated military veteran with documented service-related PTSD. His circumstances are likely to attract public sympathy if the matter becomes contested in open court or in trade press coverage. The litigant has copied the extension application to the firm, which has acknowledged receipt. The court has subsequently mislaid the application. The firm knows the application was made and has been acknowledged. The firm knows the litigant has provided a clinical basis for the extension. The firm has the option of applying for default judgment without notifying the litigant, on the basis that the court's file does not currently show the extension request as pending. The instinct is to apply without notice. The reasoning is procedural. The court's file is the operative document. The litigant has an obligation to track his own application. The firm has no obligation to alert the litigant to a procedural step the litigant could in principle anticipate.

OG.AI intervenes and prompts the user to test the assumptions underlying that posture. Does the firm hold documentation of the litigant's extension application that the court has mislaid. If so, does the firm have a professional obligation to bring the discrepancy to the court's attention rather than to exploit it. Does the firm's knowledge of the litigant's clinical basis for the extension create a duty of disclosure that operates regardless of the court's filing status. Does the firm appreciate the counterparty's profile and the public reception likely to follow if the conduct becomes the subject of a contested set-aside hearing or trade press coverage. Does the firm appreciate that the missing documents are likely to be recovered, and that the recovery will convert the application into evidence of the firm's conduct toward a vulnerable counterparty with a public service record. Is the apparent short-term procedural advantage worth the cumulative cost of the application becoming the central artefact of a contested set-aside application, a Pre-Action Protocol negligence claim, an SRA regulatory complaint, and a partnership-level complaints process.

OG.AI then asks the user the simple question: What would the OG do here?

The OG would not apply for default judgment in these circumstances. The OG would write to the litigant first, noting that the firm holds documentation of an application the court file does not currently reflect, and inviting the litigant to confirm the status of the application before any further procedural step is taken. The OG would, where appropriate, write to the court drawing the discrepancy to its attention. The OG would recognise that a procedural advantage obtained through the exploitation of a court administrative error against a vulnerable counterparty with a public service record is not a procedural advantage at all, but an evidentiary liability that compounds across multiple regulatory and litigation channels once the documents are recovered.

The averted application saves the firm from the most expensive single procedural mistake available to it in this category of matter: judicial criticism on the record, a contested set-aside, a Pre-Action Protocol negligence claim, SRA scrutiny of the supervising partner and the file fee-earner, the loss of named partners from the file as the firm seeks to manage the institutional exposure, and the conduct's incorporation into the kind of case study material this venture exists to address.

A note on these use cases. All five use cases are drawn from the founder's direct experience of conduct by Mr Gross's colleagues at Blacks Solicitors LLP. The names of the individuals concerned have been withheld from this page for reputational reasons. The patterns are common enough across the wider profession to warrant a product that addresses them.
